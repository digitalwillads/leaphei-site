Homeownership Guides

Retirement runway: equity as cash flow

For most Americans approaching retirement, the home is the largest asset on the personal balance sheet, often by a wide margin. Whether and how to convert some of that equity into spendable cash is one of the consequential financial decisions of the next decade of life. The answer depends on a specific risk most retirees have never heard of by name: sequence-of-returns risk.

The median net worth of an American household headed by someone aged 65 to 74 is roughly $410,000, according to the most recent Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances. About 60 percent of that figure is held in primary-residence equity. The retirement portfolio, the part the financial industry typically talks about, is the smaller piece. For a substantial slice of retirees, the home is not just an asset on the balance sheet. It is the balance sheet.

This creates a planning problem that most pre-retirement advice underestimates. A retirement income strategy that ignores home equity is ignoring most of the available capital. A strategy that taps it the wrong way can permanently damage the household's financial position, often without the retiree noticing for several years.

What sequence-of-returns risk actually is

If you withdraw a fixed amount each year from an investment portfolio over a 30-year retirement, the order in which your investment returns arrive matters enormously, even if the average return is identical. A portfolio that loses 20 percent in year one and then averages 7 percent for the next 29 years ends in a very different place than one that averages 7 percent for 29 years and then loses 20 percent in year 30. The first portfolio is often exhausted by year 22. The second portfolio finishes with several hundred thousand dollars to spare.

The reason is mechanical. Withdrawals taken during a down market sell shares at depressed prices, which leaves fewer shares to participate in the eventual recovery. Two years of bad returns at the start of retirement, even if followed by a long bull market, can shave a decade off the portfolio's lifespan. This is sequence-of-returns risk, and the financial planning literature has spent the past 20 years building strategies specifically to manage it.

Why home equity is the natural hedge

One of the most studied solutions is what researchers call a "buffer asset strategy." During years when the market is down, the retiree pauses portfolio withdrawals and draws income from a different source uncorrelated with stock prices. The portfolio stays intact, recovers, and returns to providing income once markets stabilize. Home equity is one of the few buffer assets most retirees actually have in sufficient size.

The classic vehicle for this is a Home Equity Conversion Mortgage, the federally insured reverse mortgage. Wade Pfau's research, published over the past decade, has demonstrated that retirees who establish a reverse mortgage line of credit at the start of retirement and draw on it during down years end up with substantially larger portfolio balances 30 years later than retirees who rely on the portfolio alone. The mechanism works. The product itself, however, has real disadvantages.

The trouble with reverse mortgages

A reverse mortgage accrues interest on the drawn balance throughout the loan's life. The interest compounds, often at rates near or above 8 percent, against a balance that grows with each draw. Over 20 or 25 years, the compound effect can consume most of the home's equity, leaving little for heirs or for the retiree's own move-out costs if assisted living becomes necessary later. The fees at origination are also substantial, typically 2 to 4 percent of the home's value, which compares unfavorably to most alternatives.

The reverse mortgage is also restrictive. Both spouses must remain in the home as the primary residence. Failure to maintain property taxes, homeowners insurance, or basic upkeep can trigger acceleration of the loan. For many retirees these terms are workable. For others, particularly those who want flexibility about where they live in their late 70s or early 80s, the constraints chafe.

The downsizing math, honestly

The conventional advice is to downsize. Sell the four-bedroom suburban house, buy a two-bedroom condo, and free up several hundred thousand dollars in equity to supplement retirement income. This works in some markets and fails in others. The transaction costs alone, broker commissions, transfer taxes, moving costs, and the renovation that almost always becomes necessary at the new place, typically consume 8 to 14 percent of the gross sale price. On a $600,000 home, that is $48,000 to $84,000 in one-time costs.

The replacement housing is also often more expensive per square foot than the home being sold, because newer construction, single-floor layouts, and accessibility features command premiums that did not exist in the original purchase. In hot urban condo markets, a downsize from a suburban single-family home often nets out at less than $150,000 of freed equity once the replacement is priced realistically. For homeowners deeply rooted in their community, with adult children nearby and an established social fabric, the non-financial costs of moving compound the financial ones.

Where a home equity investment fits

An HEI sits between the reverse mortgage and the downsize. It provides a lump sum that can fund a buffer reserve or directly supplement income, without monthly payments, without compounding interest, and without requiring a move. Unlike a reverse mortgage, the cost of capital is tied to the home's appreciation rather than a compounding interest rate, which in flat or modestly appreciating markets results in materially lower total cost over a 10 to 15 year horizon. Unlike a downsize, the retiree stays in the home, in the neighborhood, and in the network.

The trade-offs are also real. An HEI on a home that appreciates strongly will cost more at settlement than a same-sized reverse mortgage would have in a market where rates stayed moderate. The product also has a defined term, typically 10, 15, or up to 30 years, after which a settlement event is required. For a 70-year-old retiree planning a 25-year retirement, a 30-year HEI on Leap's longer-term product can cover the relevant window cleanly. For an 80-year-old, the term and the life expectancy align even more tightly.

How to evaluate the choice

The honest answer is that no single product is right for every retiree. The variables that matter are the size of the existing portfolio relative to spending needs, the household's tolerance for market volatility, the local housing market's appreciation outlook, the value placed on staying in the current home, and the importance of leaving equity to heirs. A retiree with a $1.5 million portfolio and a $700,000 home has different optimal answers than a retiree with a $200,000 portfolio and a $900,000 home, even though both might describe themselves as "house rich."

The mistake worth avoiding is treating home equity as untouchable on principle, while letting market volatility consume a portfolio that could have been preserved by drawing carefully from the home for a few critical years. The mistake equally worth avoiding is touching home equity carelessly, with a product whose long-run cost structure was not actually understood at origination.
