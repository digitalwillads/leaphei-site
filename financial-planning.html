Financial Planning

Financial Wellness,
designed for you.

Our Financial Wellness Series covers 8 topics, starting with “How Money Actually Works.” We help you explore Credit, Budgeting, Debt, and a range of topics to guide you on your journey to financial prosperity.

This curriculum was designed for people who feel locked out of the financial system — whether due to low income, past credit problems, debt, or simply never having been taught how money and our financial system works. Each module builds on the previous, moving from foundational understanding to active skill-building and long-term wealth creation. However, you can take them in order or pick & choose your topics — it’s up to you.

01
Foundation

How money actually works

The system, the rules, and who benefits

Core Topics

  • How banks create money through lending — and why that matters to you
  • The difference between assets, liabilities, income, and net worth
  • How inflation erodes purchasing power over time
  • Why financial institutions are designed the way they are
  • How predatory financial products target low-income households

What you’ll learn

How to recognize the difference between products that build wealth vs. products that extract it — before signing anything.

02
Credit

Understanding credit

Scores, reports, and how to take control

Core Topics

  • What a credit report contains and how to read it
  • How FICO scores are calculated (payment history, utilization, length, mix, inquiries)
  • How to get your free reports from AnnualCreditReport.com
  • Disputing errors — the legal process and how to actually do it
  • How negative items age off your report (7-year rule, bankruptcies at 10 years)
  • Secured cards, credit-builder loans, and becoming an authorized user

What you’ll learn

How to pull and audit your own credit report, identify any errors, and begin a concrete plan to improve your score by 50+ points in 12 months.

03
Budgeting

Budgeting without shame

Making a plan that works on any income

Core Topics

  • Why traditional budget advice often fails people with irregular income
  • The “pay yourself first” method vs. zero-based budgeting
  • Tracking spending without apps (envelope method, simple spreadsheet)
  • Understanding fixed vs. variable expenses and where cuts are actually possible
  • What to do when income doesn’t cover necessities — triage and prioritization
  • Building a bare-bones budget vs. a goal-oriented budget

What you’ll learn

How to create a written monthly spending plan in under 30 minutes using only pen and paper, and identify one area to redirect $25–$50/month toward a goal.

04
Debt

Debt — understanding and escaping it

Interest, traps, and real payoff strategies

Core Topics

  • How compound interest works against you — seeing the real cost of a minimum payment
  • Payday loans, rent-to-own, and buy-here-pay-here auto: what they really cost
  • The avalanche vs. snowball payoff method — which one works for your psychology
  • Negotiating with creditors and debt collectors (your rights under the FDCPA)
  • Understanding statute of limitations on debt by state
  • When bankruptcy might be the right tool — Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13 overview
  • Debt consolidation vs. debt settlement: the real difference

What you’ll learn

An approach for listing every debt with the balance, interest rate, and minimum payment — then map a realistic payoff order and calculate an actual freedom date.

05
Banking

Banking and saving with lower incomes

Building a safety net when there’s very little left

Core Topics

  • How to open a bank account with ChexSystems history (second-chance accounts)
  • Credit unions vs. banks vs. online banks — real differences in fees and access
  • How overdraft fees work and how to eliminate them
  • The $1,000 starter emergency fund — why this single goal changes everything
  • High-yield savings accounts: what they are and who qualifies
  • Safe places to save if you can’t get a bank account (prepaid cards, USPS accounts)

What you’ll learn

How to open or switch to a fee-free account and automate a transfer — even $5/week — to a separate savings bucket.

06
Resources

Income, benefits, and public resources

What you’re owed and how to access it

Core Topics

  • Overview of SNAP, Medicaid, CHIP, WIC, LIHEAP, and housing assistance
  • The Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) — one of the most valuable and under-claimed benefits
  • Free tax filing options (VITA, IRS Free File) and how refunds can be leveraged
  • Side income strategies: gig work, selling, childcare — and how taxes change
  • Income-driven repayment for student loans and forgiveness programs
  • Community resources: food banks, utility assistance, nonprofit credit counseling

What you’ll learn

How to identify at least 2 benefits or credits you may qualify for and take the first step to apply or verify eligibility this month.

07
Protection

Protecting what you have

Insurance, scams, and legal rights

Core Topics

  • Why renters insurance matters and how to get it for under $15/month
  • How to compare auto insurance and avoid being overcharged (credit-based insurance scoring)
  • Scams that specifically target financially vulnerable people — and the red flags
  • Identity theft: how it happens, how to freeze your credit for free, how to recover
  • Tenant rights and protections around eviction and security deposits
  • Your rights under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act and Fair Credit Reporting Act

What you’ll learn

How to place a free credit freeze at all three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) and know the two consumer protection laws you can cite if contacted by a collector.

Growth

Building wealth

Long-term thinking, even when it feels out of reach

Core Topics

  • How compound interest works for you — and why starting small still matters
  • Employer 401(k) match: the closest thing to free money that exists
  • Roth IRA basics: how to open one, contribution limits, why low-income earners benefit most
  • Homeownership as a wealth-building tool — and when it isn’t
  • The racial and systemic wealth gap — history and current realities
  • Intergenerational wealth: life insurance, wills, and beneficiary designations
  • Community wealth models: credit unions, CDFIs, and cooperative ownership

How to open one tax-advantaged account — even if you can only contribute $25 — and name a beneficiary. That act alone puts you ahead of most Americans.
