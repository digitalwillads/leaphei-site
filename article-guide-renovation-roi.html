When a renovation pays for itself, and when it doesn't

The remodeling industry sells the idea that any improvement adds value. The resale data tells a more complicated story. Some projects return more than they cost, some return a meaningful fraction, and some return almost nothing. Knowing which is which before you spend $80,000 is the difference between a renovation that builds equity and one that quietly erodes it.

Every year, the trade publication Remodeling releases its Cost vs. Value Report, which tracks the average construction cost and the average resale-value contribution of two dozen common projects across more than 100 metropolitan markets. The 2025 edition tells a story that should change how most homeowners think about renovation budgets. Of the projects studied, only three returned more than 100 percent of their cost on average. About half returned between 60 and 90 percent. Several returned below 50 percent. The category averages mask enormous regional variation, but the broad shape of the data is consistent year over year, and consistent with what real estate appraisers report from the field.

Projects that reliably recoup their cost

Garage door replacement, exterior siding replacement with fiber cement or stone veneer, and steel entry door replacement consistently lead the list, often returning 100 to 190 percent of cost. The pattern is not accidental. These are visible, exterior, curb-appeal projects with modest absolute costs, usually $3,000 to $25,000. Buyers register them in the first three seconds of seeing the house, and appraisers credit them in full because comparable sales data shows they accelerate sale timelines and reduce price negotiation.

A minor kitchen remodel, defined as new cabinet fronts, countertops, flooring, sink, and faucet without changing the footprint, recoups roughly 85 to 95 percent of cost on average and significantly more in markets where the dominant housing stock has dated kitchens. A homeowner in the Pacific Northwest or coastal Northeast can often expect a $30,000 minor kitchen update to add $32,000 or more in appraised value.

Projects that recoup most but not all of their cost

A midrange bathroom remodel ($25,000 to $40,000) returns 65 to 75 percent on average. A wood deck addition returns 50 to 70 percent. Window replacement returns 60 to 70 percent, with vinyl edging out wood in cost recovery but losing in markets that prize architectural authenticity. New roofing returns 60 to 65 percent on a straight resale calculation, though buyers heavily penalize visibly aged roofs, which means the practical return is higher once you account for the speed of sale.

The pattern in this group is that the work is substantive, the absolute dollar amounts are larger, and the personal-taste component of the design choices begins to matter. A neutral, market-standard finish recoups more than a bespoke one. Granite outperforms quartzite at resale in most price tiers, not because it is a better surface but because more buyers recognize it.

Projects that lose money on day one

Upscale master suite additions, sunroom additions, and high-end primary bathroom remodels routinely return 45 to 60 percent of cost. A $250,000 master suite addition typically adds $130,000 to $150,000 in appraised value. Backyard pool installations in cold-weather markets often return less than 40 percent and in some metros are net-negative to appraised value once maintenance assumptions are priced in. Highly customized finishes, dedicated home theaters, wine cellars, and built-in audio systems return almost nothing at resale because the next buyer does not share the original owner's specific use case.

None of this means these projects are wrong. They may be exactly right for a homeowner planning to stay 20 years and enjoy the result every day. But framing them as investments is a category error. They are consumption, paid for in equity rather than monthly income.

Regional variation matters more than category

The same project can be a 110 percent return in one market and a 60 percent return three states away. The dominant variables are local construction cost, the age and condition of the surrounding housing stock, and what buyers in that price tier expect as a baseline. A kitchen remodel in Boston returns better than the national average because most of the housing stock predates 1980 and dated kitchens are the rule. The same remodel in a 2010s-built Phoenix subdivision returns less, because comparable sales already have current kitchens and the upgrade does not differentiate.

Energy efficiency improvements have flipped over the past decade. Heat pump conversions, attic insulation, and modern HVAC systems used to return 40 to 60 percent of cost. In markets with high energy prices and active utility rebate programs, they now routinely return 80 to 110 percent on a resale basis, plus monthly savings during ownership. California, Massachusetts, New York, and parts of the Pacific Northwest are leading this shift.

How to think about funding the project

If the renovation you are considering is in the top tier of returns, the funding decision is straightforward: the project will likely cover the cost of borrowing. If it is in the middle tier, the funding source starts to matter, because a $40,000 renovation that returns $28,000 at sale can easily lose the rest in interest carry if it is funded with a HELOC held for 12 years. If it is in the bottom tier, you are paying for a lifestyle choice rather than an investment, and the cheapest possible carrying cost is the right priority.

This is one of the cases where a home equity investment can shift the math in the homeowner's favor. Because there is no monthly carrying cost during the term, the comparison between project return and funding cost simplifies considerably. The renovation either adds to home value, in which case the investor shares the upside, or it does not, in which case the homeowner has consumed equity without compounding the loss with interest.

The lesson from a decade of Cost vs. Value data is the same one professional house flippers learned long ago: spend on the projects buyers reward, fund them in a way that does not erode the return, and treat everything else as a lifestyle decision priced honestly.
