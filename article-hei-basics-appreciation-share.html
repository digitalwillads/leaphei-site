HEI Basics · 03

How appreciation share is calculated

At settlement, you owe the investor two things: the original investment, returned in full, plus a previously agreed percentage of the home's appreciation since origination. The percentage is fixed when the contract is signed. The dollar amount depends entirely on what happens to your home's value during the term.

The repayment formula on a home equity investment is genuinely simple, and that simplicity is the point. There is no compounding, no rate sheet, no margin over an index, and no escalator clause. Two numbers determine what you owe at settlement: the original investment amount, which never changes, and the appreciation share percentage, which also never changes. Multiply the percentage by the appreciation, add the principal, and that is the repayment.

The two numbers that drive everything

The first number is the investment amount itself. This is the lump sum that lands in your account at closing. At Leap it ranges from $30,000 to $300,000, capped at roughly 25 percent of the home's appraised value. The investor returns exactly this amount at settlement, no more and no less, as the principal portion of the repayment.

The second number is the appreciation share, sometimes called the investor's share or the share percentage. This is the slice of future appreciation the investor receives. The percentage is set at origination based on the size of the investment, the term length, your credit profile, and the regional housing market. For most Leap contracts it falls between 25 and 50 percent. A larger investment as a fraction of home value typically carries a higher share. A longer term typically carries a higher share. A home in a market with steady, predictable appreciation typically carries a lower share than one in a volatile market.

The formula

At settlement, an independent appraiser determines the home's current market value. The appreciation is calculated as that current value minus the starting appraised value at origination. The repayment is the original investment plus the appreciation share percentage multiplied by the appreciation. If the appreciation is zero or negative, the appreciation share portion is zero, and only the principal is repaid. In Leap's standard product, a sufficiently large decline reduces the principal repayment as well, on a one-for-one basis with the decline.

Scenario one: a modest appreciation market

Consider a homeowner in Cleveland with a home appraised at $260,000 at origination. Leap invests $50,000 with a 35 percent appreciation share, on a 10-year term. The homeowner uses the funds to pay off $42,000 in credit card debt and put the remainder toward a furnace replacement.

Eight years later, the homeowner accepts a job in Charlotte and lists the home. The sale closes at $315,000. Appreciation is $55,000. The repayment is $50,000 in principal plus 35 percent of $55,000, or $19,250. Total repayment: $69,250. The homeowner walks away from the closing table with $245,750 of equity, having had no monthly payment on the HEI for the entire eight years and having paid off the cards in the first month.

An equivalent HELOC at 8.5 percent over the same eight years, with interest-only payments, would have cost the homeowner $34,000 in interest plus the original $50,000, for a total carrying cost of $84,000. The HEI was cheaper by roughly $15,000 in this case because Cleveland's housing market appreciated at a moderate pace.

Scenario two: a strong appreciation market

Now consider a homeowner in Phoenix with a $480,000 home, taking the same $50,000 investment at the same 35 percent share. The homeowner uses the funds for a kitchen and main-floor renovation. The local market runs hot. Seven years later, the homeowner refinances rather than sells, and an appraisal comes in at $720,000. Appreciation is $240,000. The repayment is $50,000 in principal plus 35 percent of $240,000, or $84,000. Total repayment: $134,000.

In this case, the homeowner's all-in cost was higher than the HELOC alternative would have been. A $50,000 HELOC at 8.5 percent over the same seven years would have totaled around $79,750. The HEI was the more expensive choice in absolute dollars, by roughly $54,000. But the homeowner also avoided $30,000 in cumulative monthly interest payments during those seven years, which were instead available for other uses, and the renovation likely contributed meaningfully to the appreciation that drove the repayment up in the first place. The trade-off was real, and worth thinking about in advance.

Scenario three: a declining market

A homeowner in a Florida coastal market takes a $75,000 investment on a $500,000 home, with a 30 percent appreciation share and a 10-year term. A regional repricing event tied to insurance availability and climate risk drives the home's appraised value down. At year nine, the homeowner sells for $470,000, a $30,000 decline.

Under Leap's downside-sharing terms, the repayment is reduced from the $75,000 principal by the amount of the decline, on a one-for-one basis up to a defined floor. In this case the repayment falls to $45,000. The homeowner returns $45,000 of the original $75,000 and keeps the rest. A HELOC in the same situation would have required the full $75,000 back plus all accumulated interest.

What to take away

The appreciation-share structure shifts cost from the present into the future and ties it to your home's market performance. In flat or declining markets, the math favors the HEI by a wide margin. In moderate markets, the HEI is usually competitive with or cheaper than a HELOC. In strongly appreciating markets, the HEI's total cost can exceed a HELOC's, but the homeowner has paid nothing month-to-month during the term and absorbed none of the rate risk.

The best way to know which case applies to you is to run the actual numbers against a realistic range of appreciation scenarios for your specific market and your specific time horizon.
