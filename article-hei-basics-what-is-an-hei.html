HEI Basics · 01

What is a home equity investment?

A home equity investment is a contract, not a loan. You receive a lump sum of cash today in exchange for a share of your home's future appreciation, settled when you sell, refinance, or reach the end of the agreed term. There is no monthly payment, no interest rate, and nothing on your credit report.

If you have spent any time researching ways to pull cash out of your home, you have probably bumped into the same three options: a home equity line of credit, a cash-out refinance, or a second mortgage. All three have the same underlying structure. A lender gives you money today, and you give them back a monthly payment plus interest until the balance is gone. Your house is collateral. Miss enough payments and the lender forecloses.

A home equity investment, sometimes called an HEI or a home equity agreement, is a different kind of arrangement. Instead of borrowing money against your home, you sell a slice of its future. An investor like Leap pays you a lump sum now, and in exchange you agree that when your home eventually changes hands, the investor receives the original amount back plus a previously agreed share of whatever appreciation has accumulated since the contract began. If your home appreciates by $200,000 over the next decade and your appreciation share is 30 percent, the investor gets $60,000 on top of the principal. If your home does not appreciate at all, the investor gets only the principal back. That is the whole structure.

What the contract actually says

When you sign an HEI, you are agreeing to four things. First, you will continue to live in the home as your primary residence for the duration of the term. Second, you will keep the home in reasonable condition and stay current on your existing mortgage, property taxes, and homeowners insurance. Third, when the contract ends, you will repay the original investment plus the agreed appreciation share, calculated against a current appraisal. Fourth, you give the investor a lien on the property so that the repayment is enforceable. The lien is recorded in the same place a second mortgage would be, but it does not behave like one. There is no amortization schedule, no payment due date, and no interest accruing in the background.

Terms vary by provider. Leap offers terms of 10, 15, and up to 30 years. Hometap and Unison generally write 10-year agreements. Point goes out to 30. Within each term, you can settle the contract at any point by selling the home, refinancing into a new mortgage that pays the investor off, or simply writing a check from savings. There is no prepayment penalty at Leap, though some competitors charge one in the first few years.

Why people choose an HEI over a loan

The most common reason is cash flow. A homeowner with $250,000 of equity who wants $75,000 in cash has three realistic paths. A HELOC at today's prime-plus rates of around 8.5 percent will add roughly $640 in monthly interest payments before any principal repayment. A cash-out refinance on a mortgage originally taken out at 3.25 percent will reset the entire loan at something close to 7 percent, costing tens of thousands in additional interest over the life of the loan. An HEI of $75,000 at a 30 percent appreciation share creates no monthly payment at all. The household's budget the month after closing looks exactly like the month before.

The second reason is qualification. HEI providers underwrite primarily on equity and home condition rather than income and credit score. Self-employed homeowners with strong equity but uneven 1099 income, retirees whose monthly income has dropped on paper, and homeowners working through credit recovery often cannot qualify for a HELOC at any rate. Many of them can qualify for an HEI.

The third reason is risk distribution. A HELOC must be repaid in full no matter what happens to the housing market. If your home loses value during the term, you still owe the full balance. An HEI shares that downside. If the home appraises lower at settlement than it did at origination, the repayment is reduced. In some products, including Leap's, a severe decline can pull the repayment below the original principal.

What an HEI is not

An HEI is not a reverse mortgage. Reverse mortgages are loans, regulated under HUD's Home Equity Conversion Mortgage program, available only to homeowners 62 and older, and they accrue interest that compounds against the home's equity over time. An HEI has none of those characteristics. There is no age requirement, no interest, and no compounding.

An HEI is also not a sale-leaseback, in which a company buys your home outright and rents it back to you. With an HEI, you remain the owner of record. Your name stays on the deed. You decide when to sell, how to renovate, whether to refinance, and what to do with the property. The investor has a financial interest in the appreciation of an asset you continue to control.
